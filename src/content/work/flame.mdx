## The challenge

Flame was created to bring consistency across a large, multi-country banking ecosystem, where design coherence isn't just a UX concern, but a trust signal. Users make real financial decisions through these interfaces. The challenge wasn't only visual consistency, but adoption: a global system that felt too rigid would simply be ignored. The core problem was finding the right balance between standardization and autonomy, allowing teams to move fast locally without fragmenting the platform at scale.

## My role

I joined Flame as part of the core design team and later became responsible for the system and the team. My role quickly evolved from contributing to the system to leading its strategy, governance and evolution, working closely with designers, engineers and country stakeholders, and directly with Santander's Chief Design Officer to align Flame's direction with the bank's global product strategy. I was responsible for defining the system model, driving adoption across markets, facilitating alignment workshops, and making key structural decisions to keep the system scalable and sustainable over time. Operating across craft, teams and executive leadership has been a constant in my career, from Nurun and Caja Rural to Santander and CaixaBank.

> Flame was a strategic effort to standardize what mattered most while keeping the organization moving.

## Designing for adoption first

<figure class="shot"><img src="/work/flame/closed-system.webp" alt="A closed, rigid set of UI components" loading="lazy" /><figcaption>The trap to avoid: a closed, rigid component set</figcaption></figure>

Flame didn't start as a strict, centralized system. From the beginning, the approach prioritized adoption over control, knowing that a global system imposed top-down would fail. A tiered model was introduced, with a global foundation and local layers that allowed countries to adapt the system to their needs. This flexibility helped teams perceive Flame as something usable and realistic, rather than an abstract set of rules.

<figure class="shot"><img src="/work/flame/sticker-sheet.webp" alt="Flame component sticker sheet" loading="lazy" /><figcaption>A tiered component system: global foundation, local layers</figcaption></figure>

> The system was intentionally designed as an agreement, not as a constraint.

## From building a system to enabling teams

As adoption grew, the system required less direct production and more enablement. At this point, responsibility for the team shifted focus toward helping countries consume, adapt and extend the system correctly. The challenge was no longer designing components, but designing the conditions for the system to scale: guidance instead of enforcement, support instead of control, feedback loops instead of assumptions.

> Flame started to behave like a product platform, owned by a small core team but operated by many.

## Designing through consensus: the European tier

A major turning point came with the creation of a shared European tier, co-designed by several countries. Instead of each market evolving independently, work focused on defining shared needs, resolving differences and making decisions collectively. This process required deep alignment, facilitation and explicit trade-offs, but the result was a system that was more complete and more robust than the original global layer.

> The European tier eventually became better than the original reference, proving that the system could evolve through collaboration, not hierarchy.

## Breaking the system to make it sustainable (Flame 4.0)

When the client requested that the European tier become the new global reference, it became clear that simply promoting it would increase technical and design debt. Instead, a deliberate decision was made to introduce a breaking change and rebuild the system from the ground up as Flame 4.0. This was used as an opportunity to redesign the governance model, redefine contribution rules, and rethink the system architecture. The transition moved from a flexibility-first, tier-based approach to a hybrid model: a shared core with foundations and essential components protected centrally, with flexibility shifted to higher-level compositions and patterns.

> The goal was no longer to maximize freedom, but to protect consistency where it mattered most, while still allowing teams to build on top of a solid, shared base.
