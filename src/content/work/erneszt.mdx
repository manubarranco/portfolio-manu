## The challenge

I've always noticed a moment that everyone around me goes through, and that I've been through myself more than once. A moment of open possibilities and uncomfortable uncertainty. What should I do next? It usually appears at transition points, after a job, a project, or simply when something stops feeling right. The problem isn't a lack of options, but the opposite: too many paths, and no clear signal of which one to follow. Most people look outward (job listings, advice, inspiration), but that rarely solves the real issue. What's missing is clarity about your own story: what you've been building, the patterns behind your decisions, and what actually drives you. Without that, moving forward feels like guessing.

## My role

I led the project end to end, from identifying the problem to shaping the solution and bringing it to life. It started as a context-first exploration, where research and product ideation were co-created with AI. From there, I defined the narrative, designed the experience, and structured the context and instructions needed to orchestrate the different agents that coded the app. Erneszt is the result of a hands-on, iterative approach, where thinking, making and testing happened continuously, without clear boundaries between roles.

## The importance of being Erneszt

There's a particular kind of design work that doesn't start with a brief, a Figma file, or a sprint plan. It starts with a question you can't stop thinking about. For me, that question was this: why do so many people systematically undervalue what they already are?

Not because they lack talent or ambition. But because they're too close to themselves to see it clearly.

That observation became the seed of Erneszt: an AI-native product designed to surface what professionals already know about themselves but can't fully claim. This is the story of how I conceptualized it, and why the process matters as much as the outcome.

### Starting with the problem, not the solution

The first thing I did was resist the urge to design anything.

Instead, I spent time mapping the real problem space. The market was full of AI coaching platforms (BetterUp, CoachHub, Rocky.ai), but almost all of them were B2B, enterprise-facing, and structured around ongoing performance improvement. They assumed the user already knew what they wanted to work on. What was missing was something that helped people figure out who they actually were before prescribing any direction.

Personality tests like 16Personalities or StrengthsFinder came closer emotionally: they produce that moment of recognition, the "this is me" click that makes people share results compulsively. But they put users into generic categories. The specificity that makes a result feel truly personal was absent.

The gap I identified: a product that combined the emotional mechanics of viral personality tests with the genuine depth of a skilled coach, and made the result specific enough to be undeniable.

### Defining the emotional architecture before the product architecture

Most product design processes move from problem to solution. I moved from problem to emotional experience first, and only then to product structure. This sequence matters enormously for AI-native products, because the AI doesn't just deliver features, it delivers a feeling. If you haven't designed the feeling, you haven't designed the product.

I mapped three emotional moments that the experience had to produce in sequence. The first is **Recognition**, the interior click: "this is me and I wasn't seeing it." This is where the real value is created. Everything else is in service of this moment. The second is **Belonging**, the discovery that there's a specific category you fit into, with your particular nuances, not a generic archetype shared by millions. The third is **Sharing**, the exterior moment: "this explains who I am and now the world knows it." This circles the loop back to Recognition: first I recognized myself, now I want others to do the same. This is the viral engine. But it only works if the first two moments were real.

This emotional sequence became the design constraint that governed every subsequent decision. Any feature, any interaction, any piece of copy that didn't serve these three moments in order was out.

### Conceptualizing the character before designing the interface

The product needed a concept that could carry the user through all three emotional moments without breaking the spell at any point. That meant finding something that could hold authority, mystery, and warmth simultaneously. Something that felt like it could see what you can't see about yourself.

We explored the territory of figures who do exactly that. The mentalist, who reads you before you've said anything. The oracle, who knows without being told. And then we landed on a reference that felt almost too good: the Sorting Hat from Harry Potter. A mechanism that looks inside you and tells you where you belong, with total authority and zero sentimentality. The emotional mechanics were exactly right.

But the Sorting Hat is magic. And this isn't magic, it's technology. It's precision. It's a system that works because it's been engineered to work, not because it's enchanted. That distinction mattered enormously for the product's credibility. If users felt they were being mystified, the moment the illusion cracked, everything would fall apart. The product needed to feel like it had earned its authority through accumulated observation, not borrowed it from fantasy.

That's how we arrived at the automaton. A machine. Specifically, a machine like Zoltar in the 80s movie *Big*: a coin-operated fortune teller that has been standing in fairgrounds for decades, watching people, and telling them true things in exchange for a quarter.

But Zoltar has its own problem: it's still performing magic. It gives you a fortune card that could mean anything to anyone. We needed something more specific, more grounded. So we invented the lore.

The automaton was born twice. The first time, it was a fairground attraction, built for spectacle, designed to impress. The second time, it was taken apart and rebuilt by an engineer who gave it something the original never had: genuine analytical and interpretive capability. The ability to read a professional trajectory the way a master clockmaker reads a mechanism: not what it looks like, but how it actually works and why. That lore made the character coherent. It explained why the automaton speaks the way it does: little, precisely, without flattery. It explained the aesthetic: mechanical, aged, precise. And it gave the product something most AI products lack entirely: a reason to exist that predates the user's interaction with it.

Once the lore existed, everything else followed from it. The visual language became legible: what does a machine that was born twice look like? What is its relationship to Art Nouveau ornamentation, to fairground typography, to the warm amber light of Edison bulbs? The naming criteria became clear. The voice and the conversational rules became derivable from the character rather than invented from scratch. The interface decisions (the vitrine that frames the automaton, the card that emerges from the slot with each beat of the output) stopped being aesthetic choices and became narrative ones.

> This is the design principle that AI-native products most consistently violate: they build the intelligence first and the character never. Without it, you have a capable system that nobody believes in.

### Building a five-phase conversation architecture

Once the character was defined, I designed the conversational system not as a single prompt but as five distinct phases, each with its own role.

**Phase 1, silent analysis.** The automaton reads the user's LinkedIn profile or CV and produces a structured internal map: trajectory patterns, core strengths, the blind spot, tensions, and the architecture of the conversation ahead. The user never sees this. It's the intelligence that makes everything that follows feel prescient.

**Phase 2, the mentalist moment.** Three or four observations delivered in crescendo, moving from concrete to abstract, culminating in a single punch line that synthesizes something the user has never heard said about themselves quite that way. The goal is to produce the "how does it know that?" reaction that opens the user emotionally to everything that follows.

**Phase 3, a Socratic conversation** where the automaton takes initiative. The questions are specific and purposeful: excavating, connecting, reframing. The conversation has no predetermined length. It ends when conditions are met, not when a timer runs out.

**Phase 4 constructs the narrative arc silently:** a three-movement structure of Recognition, Expansion, and Permission, with a beat-by-beat blueprint that specifies content, rhythm, pause points, and emotional register for each moment.

**Phase 5 delivers the output** as a sequential experience, not a document. Each beat is revealed one at a time. The user advances when ready. The final beat arrives alone and the automaton stops. Silence is the correct ending.

This architecture took weeks to design properly. The temptation to start with a single prompt and iterate was strong. I resisted it because I understood that the quality of the output depends entirely on the quality of the architecture that produces it. Rushing to build before thinking is the most expensive mistake in AI product design.

### Designing for virality through specificity, not mechanics

The sharing mechanics of the product are not gamification. They are a direct consequence of the output's quality.

Personality tests go viral because the result feels true and shareable. The difference with this product is that the result isn't a category, it's a specific insight about a specific person. That specificity is what makes it undeniable to share. You're not saying "I'm an INFJ." You're saying "this machine told me something about myself that I recognized immediately and had never heard articulated before."

The product has two shareable moments by design: the punch at the end of Phase 2, impulsive and surprise-driven, and the final output card after Phase 5, identity-driven and more durable. Each requires a different format and a different motivation to share. Both were designed explicitly as part of the experience, not added afterwards as a marketing layer.

### Context-first development: building the brief before the build

Before writing a single line of code, I produced four documents that would govern development: a product definition covering vision, emotional mechanics, and business model; a prompt architecture document containing the five complete prompts with full specifications; a project brief establishing the technical stack and MVP scope; and a structured product backlog with seven epics and twenty user stories, each with acceptance criteria.

This is what context-first development means in practice. It means that when I opened a development environment, every decision had already been made by the designer, not improvised by the builder. The AI development agent had enough context to make coherent architectural decisions without me having to explain the product from scratch in every session.

The approach inverts the typical prototyping impulse, which is to start building and figure out what you're building as you go. That impulse is understandable. It feels like progress. But in AI-native products, where the intelligence is the product, building before thinking produces something that is technically functional and experientially empty.

### What this process demonstrates

Designing AI-native products requires a design discipline that most product teams haven't developed yet. It requires thinking in emotional sequences, not feature lists. It requires designing character and voice before designing interface. It requires understanding that an AI prompt is a design artifact with the same care requirements as a user flow or a component library.

The skills that made me effective here (systems thinking, emotional design, the ability to hold a complex architecture in mind across weeks of iteration) are not new skills. They're the same skills that make any designer effective. What's new is the surface on which they're applied.

Erneszt is still in development. But the conceptualization process described here produced something that most AI products lack from the start: a clear answer to the question of what it should feel like to use it. Everything else is implementation.
