## The challenge

There's a moment everyone around me goes through, and that I've been through myself more than once: too many possible paths, and no clear signal of which one to follow. It appears at transition points, after a job, a project, or when something stops feeling right. Most people look outward for the answer: job listings, advice, inspiration. What's actually missing is clarity about your own story. What you've been building, the patterns behind your decisions, what drives you. Without that, moving forward feels like guessing.

## My role

I led the project end to end, from identifying the problem to shaping the solution and bringing it to life. Research and product ideation were co-created with AI. From there, I defined the narrative, designed the experience, and structured the context and instructions that orchestrate the AI agents that coded the app. Thinking, making and testing happened continuously, without clear boundaries between roles.

## The gap

The market was full of AI coaching platforms: B2B, enterprise-facing, built around ongoing performance improvement. They assume you already know what you want to work on. Personality tests come closer emotionally: they produce the “this is me” click that makes people share results compulsively, but they put you into generic categories shared by millions.

The gap sat between the two: a product with the emotional mechanics of a viral personality test and the depth of a skilled coach, specific enough to be undeniable.

## Designing the feeling first

In an AI-native product the AI doesn't deliver features, it delivers a feeling. If you haven't designed the feeling, you haven't designed the product. So before any interface work, I mapped the three emotional moments the experience had to produce, in order, and made them the constraint that governed every decision that followed.

The automaton was born twice. First as a fairground attraction, built for spectacle. Then taken apart and rebuilt by an engineer who gave it what the original never had: genuine analytical capability, the ability to read a professional trajectory the way a master clockmaker reads a mechanism. That lore made everything else derivable, rather than invented from scratch: why it speaks little, precisely and without flattery. The aged, mechanical Art Deco aesthetic. The vitrine that frames it, the card that emerges from the slot with each beat. Interface decisions stopped being aesthetic choices and became narrative ones.

> This is the design principle that AI-native products most consistently violate: they build the intelligence first and the character never. Without it, you have a capable system that nobody believes in.

## A five-phase conversation architecture

I designed the conversational system as five phases, not one prompt. Each phase is a design artifact with its own full specification. Two of them the user never sees: they are the intelligence that makes everything else feel prescient.

This architecture took weeks to design properly. The temptation to start with a single prompt and iterate was strong. I resisted it because the quality of the output depends entirely on the quality of the architecture that produces it. Rushing to build before thinking is the most expensive mistake in AI product design.

When I opened a development environment, the AI agents had enough context to make coherent architectural decisions without me re-explaining the product in every session. The approach inverts the usual prototyping impulse, which is to start building and figure it out as you go. In AI-native products, where the intelligence is the product, building before thinking produces something technically functional and experientially empty.

## Designed to be shared

The sharing mechanics are not gamification; they are a consequence of specificity. You're not saying “I'm an INFJ”. You're saying “this machine told me something about myself I recognized immediately and had never heard articulated”. Two shareable moments exist by design: the punch at the end of the mentalist moment, impulsive and surprise-driven, and the final card, identity-driven and durable. Each has its own format and its own motivation to share.

## What this demonstrates

Designing AI-native products means thinking in emotional sequences, not feature lists. Designing character and voice before interface. Treating a prompt as a design artifact with the same care requirements as a user flow or a component library. The skills are the ones that make any designer effective; what's new is the surface they're applied to.
